# Polished results paragraph

The final comparison was performed on a cleaned raw/common-success dataset using the frequencies 5, 10, 15, 20, 40, 80 Hz. A closest-only candidate policy was used for the Main4/MPC hardware-search output, while Main5/OCP refinement was used only for success filtering and metric-hardness weight calibration. The common-success set contained 17 scenarios: s00, s02, s04, s05, s06, s07, s08, s09, s10, s11, s12, s13, s14, s15, s16, s17, s18. For each scenario and metric, the Main4 hardware value was normalized by the geometric mean across the compared frequencies, and the resulting scenario-relative ratios were combined using an OCP-hardness weighted product score.

In this cleaned comparison, 80 Hz achieved the lowest scenario-relative hardware burden score, followed by 20 Hz, 15 Hz, 40 Hz, 10 Hz, and 5 Hz. This should not be interpreted as a universal or global optimum. The component-wise ratios are the primary evidence, while the scalar score is only a compact summary. Component-wise, 80 Hz is strong in Tdot, delta, and delta-dot, while its Tw ratio is approximately neutral rather than dominant. The middle-frequency band remains close, and the detailed ordering among 20, 15, 10, and 40 Hz is sensitive to the aggregation choice.

The result also should not be interpreted as monotonic improvement with increasing frequency. The component-wise trends remain multi-metric and non-monotonic: a frequency can improve one hardware metric while worsening another. Sensitivity checks with alternative hardness-weight definitions, equal weights, aggregation choices, and coupling-adjusted weights kept 80 Hz in the leading position for this cleaned set, while 5 Hz remained consistently weak. The conclusion is therefore dataset-conditioned: 80 Hz gives the best trade-off in the cleaned common-success comparison, but the broader interpretation should emphasize component-wise trade-offs rather than a universal optimal frequency.
